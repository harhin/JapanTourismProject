Cheap Lodging


When travelling in Japan, there are many ways to save money on lodging while still being able to live in some comfort. They ranged from the unique experiences of Capsule hotels and Internet Cafes to the comfort of cheap Kanshuku or Economy hotels and Airbnb. If you would like to also have the chance to socialize while in Japan, you can possibly also check out the many hostels and dormitories then are open to travellers.



Capsule hotels

Description

A type of hotel developed in Japan that features many small bed-sized rooms known as capsules. Capsule hotels provide cheap, basic overnight accommodation for guests who do not require or who cannot afford larger, more expensive rooms offered by more conventional hotels.

Internet Cafes

Click To Learn More

Click on me to reveal more info

Description

Japan has a strong Internet cafe culture, with most serving a dual purpose as joint Internet-manga cafes. They also give access to "night packs", which allows people to sleep and shower in their facilities. Sadly, this has created a culture of "net cafe refugees" and "net cafe homeless", where people use net cafes to run away from home and sometimes the problems of life. As such, internet cafes are a cheap option, you may end up encountering some interesting stories while staying there.

Economy hotels or "Kanshuku"

Description

Similar to hostels, they tend to offer a cheap accomendation for people travelling without that much money. The difference is that, kanshuku's tend to be very japanese in style, with many of the rooms having tatami mat floors and futon beds. It is an interesting way of staying in Japan, so if you want to spice them up a bit, why not give it a try.

Description

Similar to the Kanshuku, the Ryokan could be considered the embodiment of Japanese hospitality, with a difference being the increase quality of living (as well as the accommpanying price increase). These Japanese inns offer a variety of facilities, from open hot springs to full course dinners prepared for you by the Kanshuku staff. If you wish to experience traditional Japanese hospitality, then this is the place for you!

Description

a lower-cost form of short-term shared sociable lodging where guests can rent a bed, usually a bunk bed in a dormitory, with shared use of a lounge and sometimes a kitchen. Rooms can be mixed or single-sex and have private or shared bathrooms. Private rooms may also be available, but the property must offer dormitories to be considered a hostel.
